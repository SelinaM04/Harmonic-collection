Ashtanga Yoga Chants

The practice of Ashtanga Yoga traditionally begins and ends with Sanskrit chants, which hold a significant place in the Ashtanga tradition. These chants serve as a means to express gratitude, dedication, and a sense of connection to the practice and its lineage.

Opening Chant

The opening chant is a way to pay homage to the lineage of teachers who have passed down the practice of yoga. It is a way of giving thanks to the ancient sages for their wisdom and acknowledges the profound connection between the practitioner and the teacher. The chant is seen as a way to purify the practice space and create a sacred environment for yoga.

Lyrics:

vande gurūṇāṁ caraṇāravinde
sandarśita svātma sukhāva bodhe
niḥśreyase jāṅgalikāyamāne
saṁsāra hālāhala mohaśāntyai

Translation: I bow to the lotus feet of the supreme guru
who teaches knowledge, awakening the great happiness of the self-revealed
who acts like the jungle physician
able to remove the delusion from the poison of conditioned existence.

Closing Chant

The closing chant is about universal peace and goodwill. It is a moment of reflection, expressing a wish for peace, happiness, and freedom for all beings. This chant helps to bring closure to the practice, allowing practitioners to feel a sense of harmony and connection with the world around them.

Lyrics:

svasti prajābhyaḥ paripālayantāṁ
nyāyena mārgeṇa mahīṁ mahīśāḥ
gobrāhmaṇebhyaḥ śubham astu nityaṁ
lokāḥ samastāḥ sukhino bhavantu

Translation: May all be well with mankind
May the leaders of the earth protect in every way by keeping to the right path
May there be goodness for those who know the earth to be sacred
May all the worlds be happy.

Both chants are recited in Sanskrit, the classical language of India and the language of yoga. They are seen as a way to connect more deeply with the practice, transcending the physical aspects and touching the spiritual essence of yoga. The rhythm and vibration of the chants are believed to resonate with the heart, bringing a deeper sense of peace and presence to the practitioner.
